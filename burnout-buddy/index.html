Why it exists

You may be fixing the wrong thing.

Burnout is usually treated as a personal failing. Work harder, be more resilient, manage your stress better.

But the research keeps pointing somewhere else: workload, autonomy, recognition, fairness, belonging. Many of the strongest drivers of burnout are environmental, not personal.

When people blame themselves for a system problem, they spend enormous energy trying to fix something that was never theirs to fix alone.

Burnout Buddy helps you see what you're actually carrying, what belongs to you, what belongs to the system, and what options become available once you can tell the difference.

The insight

Orientation creates relief.

Confusion is exhausting. Clarity is the rest.

Understanding doesn't always change the situation. But it changes how much energy the situation takes to carry.

There's a moment people describe, when something clicks:

"This isn't a discipline problem. It's a math problem." "This pattern has a name."

The situation hasn't changed. Their relationship to it has. Burnout Buddy is built around that moment.

Current build

A five-stage session.

01

Glow Check-In

A quick emotional read that establishes where you're starting from.

02

Challenge

You name what feels most present right now.

03

Insight

The companion surfaces an insight that helps you see the pattern, not only the feeling.

04

Action

A small, practical experiment to try. Not homework, just a bit of movement.

05

Restore

A short closing reflection that lowers the urgency and helps you leave steadier than you arrived.

Ask Burnout Buddy. At any point, you can go deeper with the companion. The cards orient; the companion helps you apply, interpret, and explore.

What we've learned

A card you keep is worth more than an exercise you finish.

01

Validation alone isn't enough. People don't only want to feel seen. They want to understand.

02

The strongest insights reveal a mechanism. The most memorable ones hand people portable language for what they're facing.

03

Agency matters more than advice. A small move someone chooses beats a better one handed to them.

04

Restore mattered more than we expected. Leaving steadier is often the point, not a nicety at the end.

05

The most valuable cards are the ones people revisit, reuse, teach, and share. Meaning deepens through use, not accumulation.
