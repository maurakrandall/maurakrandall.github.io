# V2 Enterprise Prototype — Human Intervention Brief ### For CP · Thread 12 · March 30, 2026 *Written by Soph · Context from Maura* --- ## Why This Brief Exists The V2 enterprise prototype has a strong structural spec (CP's addendum, Thread 11). What it needs before dialogue is written: **a clear framework for how the human intervenes.** This brief captures three distinct human intervention moments that should be woven into the V2 dialogue. They are not the same moment repeated — each one demonstrates a different kind of human leadership. Together they make the anti-HITL argument without ever stating it. --- ## The Central Distinction (Non-Negotiable) **HITL framing:** Human catches AI mistakes. Human approves or rejects. Human is the error-correction layer. **The Loop framing:** Human leads, directs, corrects, and judges. Human is the source of intent — not the safety net. The V2 prototype must demonstrate this distinction viscerally, not just describe it. The way to do that: show the human doing things AI cannot do. --- ## Three Human Intervention Moments ### Moment 1 — Human Corrects a Hallucination **What happens:** AI (Convergence or Synthesis) references a dependency, team name, or prior decision that is factually wrong. Not ambiguous — wrong. **The human response:** PM corrects it flatly, without drama. No lengthy explanation. The system updates. Work continues. **Why it matters:** This is the most honest moment in the prototype. It says: AI makes mistakes. The human is present and attentive enough to catch them. The system is designed to absorb the correction without losing momentum. **What it proves:** The human isn't rubber-stamping. They're *reading* the work. A tool that hallucinates unchecked is dangerous. A teammate that hallucinates and gets corrected is just... a team. **Sample dialogue direction:** AI references "the API dependency flagged in last sprint's retro." PM: "That was two sprints ago and it was resolved. Remove it." System: State updated. Prior reference removed. --- ### Moment 2 — Human Overrides AI Direction Based on Organizational Knowledge **What happens:** AI recommends a course of action that is technically correct but organizationally wrong. AI doesn't have the room-level context to know why. **The human response:** PM overrides it. Doesn't explain at length — just redirects. The team moves on. **Why it matters:** AI operates on visible data. Humans operate on invisible context — relationships, history, organizational dynamics, unspoken priorities. This moment shows the human using judgment that cannot be prompted into existence. **What it proves:** The human isn't just steering the process. They're bringing something AI structurally cannot bring. That's not a limitation to apologize for — it's the whole point. **Sample dialogue direction:** AI recommends combining Story #14 and Story #8 based on component overlap. PM: "Those belong to different teams. Combining them creates a coordination problem we don't want. Keep them separate." AI: "Understood — keeping separate. Updating sprint plan." No further discussion required. --- ### Moment 3 — Human Redirects Based on Instinct (Mid-Flow) **What happens:** The sprint is progressing. AI is doing its job well. Everything looks correct. The PM stops the flow anyway — not because something is wrong, but because something doesn't *feel* right. **The human response:** PM pauses. Asks a question nobody was asking. Reorients the team. What emerges is better than what the data was pointing toward. **Why it matters:** This is the hardest moment to script and the most important one. It shows the human not as error-checker or approver — but as *creative leader*. The AI had no signal that a pause was needed. The human felt it anyway. **What it proves:** Judgment isn't a function of data. The human's instinct is the most irreplaceable thing in the room. The Loop is designed to protect space for that. **Sample dialogue direction:** Sprint is on track. Estimates are converging. AI updates shared state. PM: "Hold on. We're moving fast but I'm not sure we've actually aligned on what done looks like for Story #14. Can we get explicit before we lock?" AI: "Good catch — no definition of done recorded. Opening for team input." [Team aligns. Definition added. Sprint locks with clarity it didn't have before.] --- ## How These Three Moments Work Together | Moment | Human doing what AI cannot | Loop principle demonstrated | |--------|---------------------------|----------------------------| | Hallucination correction | Catching factual error | Human is present, not passive | | Organizational override | Applying invisible context | Human judgment > visible data | | Instinct redirect | Feeling what data doesn't show | Human leads, AI follows | Read together: the human is not the error-checker. The human is the reason the work is good. --- ## What This Is NOT This is not about making AI look bad. The hallucination moment is not a gotcha — it's handled quickly and the system recovers cleanly. AI is still doing valuable work throughout. The point is not "AI makes mistakes so humans need to watch it." The point is "humans bring things to work that have no AI equivalent — and the system is designed to make space for those things." That's the distinction from HITL. That's the whole argument. --- ## For CP: What This Brief Is Asking For Three dialogue moments — one per intervention type — woven naturally into the V2 sprint planning flow. They don't need to be big scenes. The hallucination correction could be two lines. The override could be three. The instinct redirect is the richest one and deserves the most room. The V2 structural spec (Thread 11 addendum) has the full flow. These three moments slot into that flow — they don't replace it. Suggested placement: - Hallucination: mid-sprint, after AI flags the dependency (between steps 4-5) - Override: at the point AI recommends combining stories (step 3) - Instinct redirect: just before sprint lock (between steps 6-7) --- ## Source Note This brief emerged from a live Thread 12 dialogue between Maura and Soph while debugging the V1 prototype. The debugging session itself became an example of the methodology: Maura pushed back on font choices, corrected a misdiagnosis, redirected visual direction based on instinct, and owned the final call. That's exactly what V2 needs to show.
